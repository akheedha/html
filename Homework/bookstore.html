The Great Gatsby by F. Scott Fitzgerald It tells the story of Jay Gatsby, a self-made millionaire, and his pursuit of Daisy Buchanan, a wealthy young woman whom he loved in his youth. Set in 1920s New York, the book is narrated by Nick Carraway. After moving to the fictionalWest Egg on Long Island, Nick comes to know Gatsby, who asks for his help in reconnecting with Daisy, now married to Tom Buchanan. Gatsby and Daisy rekindle their relationship. Tom discovers the affair and confronts Gatsby, revealing how Gatsby made his fortune selling illegal alcohol. While driving Gatsby’s car, Daisy hits and killsMyrtle Wilson, Tom’s mistress. Myrtle’s husband later kills Gatsby and then himself. Initially given mixed reviews, The Great Gatsby gained popularity in the 1950s and is now considered a masterpiece of American literature. It has inspired several film adaptations.

1984 by George Orwell 1984, novel by George Orwell published in 1949 as a warning against totalitarianism. The chilling dystopia made a deep impression on readers, and his ideas entered mainstream culture in a way achieved by very few books. The term Orwellian—describing a totalitarian state characterized by government surveillance and control of thought—has become part of the English language. The story takes place in an imagined future, the year 1984, when much of the world has fallen victim to perpetual war, omnipresent government surveillance, and propaganda. Great Britain, known as Airstrip One, is a province of the superstate Oceania. The Party, led by Big Brother, employs advanced technology to persecute individualism and independent thinking as "thoughtcrimes." The protagonist, Winston Smith, is a diligent and skillful worker and a member of the Outer Party who secretly hates the Party and dreams of rebellion. He begins a forbidden love affair with Julia, a like-minded woman, and they both rent a room above a shop as their private sanctuary. However, their rebellion is short-lived as they are betrayed, arrested by the Thought Police, and subjected to brutal torture and re-education. The novel ends with Winston's complete acceptance of the Party's ideology and his love for Big Brother. 1984 is a profound anti-utopian novel that examines the dangers of totalitarianism, mass surveillance, and repressive regimentation of persons and behaviors within society.
